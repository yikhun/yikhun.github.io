What the Evidence Points To

Evidence 1: World Cup–specific activity barely left a trace. Whether you look at the "Event" tag (promotion/bonus attribution) or the "Source" tag (traffic origin), turnover attributable to "World Cup" sits at 0.03%–0.05% of the total, and almost all of the platform's actual volume over these 35 days ran through the pre-existing "Old Event" mechanism instead. There are three possible explanations, and they're not mutually exclusive. Reach might have been too low — WC campaigns may never have surfaced in most players' actual betting path if they were confined to a promo page instead of the homepage or bet-slip flow. Attribution might be broken — players could have arrived because of the World Cup and bet accordingly, but the system's tags just didn't catch it, which would mean MKT's real contribution is understated (this is the one worth ruling out first, given how much volume "Old Event" is soaking up). Or the incentive itself wasn't compelling enough — players saw the offer and still chose the existing mechanic over it. Figuring out which one it actually is means pulling real betting records, not guessing from the aggregate numbers.

Evidence 2: paid marketing's attributable contribution comes in under 2.1%. Add up traffic attribution across SEO, Paid Ads, Social Media, KOL, and the currency-specific CM teams and you get roughly $6.55M — 2.04% of platform Valid TO (this whole analysis runs on Looker's basis; the denominator is Looker's Valid TO of $322,053,550, which becomes 2.09% against the Admin-basis authoritative number). Organic and Affiliate cover the rest — 59.2% and 38.7% respectively, plus another 0.02% booked separately as Affiliate — north of 97.9% combined. Over a 35-day World Cup window, our own paid channels just didn't show leverage that matched the size of the event. Or they did spend and it wasn't tracked properly either.

Evidence 3: daily Margin swung around rather than climbing with the tournament. Company Margin bounced between 1.3% and 5.7% across the window — 5.67% on Jun 29, down to 1.32% on Jun 23, back to 1.53% on the most recent day, Jul 16 — with nothing resembling a structural lift once the knockout stage started. This looks a lot more like ordinary baseline noise with a thin layer of World Cup traffic on top than the tournament actually reshaping how people bet.

What we'd do differently next time

Verify before concluding anything. Pull the actual betting records of players known to have engaged with World Cup activity — arrived via a WC banner, or claimed a WC-specific bonus — and check whether their Event/Source tags landed correctly. If the attribution system has a real gap, MKT's contribution could be badly understated. Fix the tracking first; don't decide the campaign failed based on numbers that might be broken.

Launch earlier next time, and put it somewhere people will actually see it. For the next major tournament (Copa América, the Euros), get campaigns live at least two weeks out, and put the entry point in the main bet-slip flow or the app homepage — not just a banner on a promo page nobody visits.

Reconcile actual spend against what got attributed. The paid share came in low, so compare real finance spend against what Looker attributed, and rule out "we spent the money, it just didn't get tracked."

Switch to an incremental metric. Next time, measure "World Cup window turnover vs. the same period last year with no major tournament" instead of "total platform turnover during the event window" — that isolates what the campaign actually added instead of drowning it in baseline volume.

The Target Itself May Never Have Been Aligned

Take this period's 35-day rate of $313.5M (Admin-basis Valid TO) and annualize it, and you land at roughly $3.27B a year. The platform's overall scale is already well above 1B — it's just that 35 days is only 9.6% of the year, so squeezing a full year's order of magnitude into one tournament window was never going to land, arithmetically, from day one. The real question isn't whether we hit the number. It's whether anyone ever agreed on what "1B" was actually supposed to measure — absolute volume during the event, or the annual lift the event drives. That question matters more than any single lens's achievement rate.

Here's what we'd do differently next time.

Align on the target before touching execution. Before the next major tournament, sit down with whoever set the "1B" goal and pin down what it actually means — absolute event-window volume, annual uplift, or just a rallying number. Then build a KPI around that answer that's actually achievable and attributable, something like "X% growth over the same period last year" or "Y% of turnover from new customers acquired during the event."

There's a zero-budget move sitting right there for Product. Make the World Cup entry point more visible on the app and site homepage during the event — that's entirely in Product's hands, no extra budget needed — and A/B test the bet-slip flow to cut drop-off. That ships faster than waiting on MKT to get more budget approved.

Fix attribution before judging anything by it. Before the next campaign goes live, make sure Event/Source tags actually cover all World Cup–related traffic, including players who came in through a WC entry point but ended up betting with a standard bonus. Right now that tracking gap makes it hard to say with any confidence what MKT actually contributed, or what Product's changes actually did.

Keep pushing localization — it's still the right call. VND, THB, INR, and CNY together are over 90% of Valid TO. Language and cultural localization around the tournament carries more weight than incremental ad spend, especially with paid teams sitting under 2.1% attributed contribution. Organic reach and product experience are what's actually driving growth here.

Split the target into stages if we take this on again. Break it into a pre-tournament baseline, the tournament window itself, and post-tournament carry-over (D7/D30 retention), each with its own achievable sub-goal. One vague headline number just gets interpreted five different ways by five different teams.

The most important finding in this section: across two independent tracking dimensions — "Event" and "Source" — turnover explicitly tagged as World-Cup-related comes in below 0.05% of the total. On the Event dimension (MYR/VND/THB World Cup tags + "Trip to WC"), the total is $131,763 (0.041%); on the Source dimension (traffic tagged "WC" + "WC activities"), the total is $98,676 (0.031%). By contrast, "Old Event" — the platform's pre-existing, non-WC-specific mechanism — carried 99.81% of turnover. This finding feeds directly into the MKT review below.

What the Evidence Points To

Evidence 1 — World Cup–specific activity left almost no measurable trace. Whether we look at the "Event" tag (promotion/bonus attribution) or the "Source" tag (traffic origin), turnover attributable to "World Cup" sits between 0.03%–0.05% of the total, while the platform's actual volume over these 35 days ran almost entirely through the pre-existing "Old Event" mechanism. Three explanations could be true at once, and each needs verification before we draw conclusions:

  1. Reach was insufficient — WC-specific campaigns may never have surfaced in most players' actual betting path (e.g. confined to a promo page rather than the homepage or bet-slip flow).
  2. Attribution is broken (undercounting) — players may have arrived because of the World Cup and bet accordingly, but the system's Event/Source tags never captured it, meaning MKT's true contribution is understated. This sits alongside the 99.81% share parked in "Old Event," and should be the first thing we rule out.
  3. The incentive design didn't move behavior — even where exposure did happen, players kept using existing mechanics instead of the WC-specific offer, suggesting the offer itself wasn't compelling enough to shift behavior.

Evidence 2 — paid marketing's attributable contribution is under 2.1%. Summing traffic attribution across SEO, Paid Ads, Social Media, KOL, and the currency-specific CM teams comes to roughly $6.55M, or 2.04% of platform Valid TO — against Organic (59.2%) and Affiliate (38.7% plus a separate 0.02% Affiliate line) at a combined 97.9%+. Over a 35-day World Cup window, our owned paid channels didn't show leverage proportionate to the size of the event — or they did spend, but face the same attribution gap.

Evidence 3 — daily Margin swung widely rather than trending up with the tournament. Company Margin oscillated between 1.3% and 5.7% across the window (a high of 5.67% on Jun 29, a low of 1.32% on Jun 23, and 1.53% on the most recent day, Jul 16), with no structural lift visible as the knockout stage began. This reads more like ordinary baseline volatility with a thin layer of World Cup traffic on top, rather than the World Cup reshaping betting behavior.

What we'd do differently next time
  • Verify before concluding: sample the actual betting records of players known to have engaged with World Cup activity (arrived via a WC banner, or claimed a WC-specific bonus) and check whether their Event/Source tags were classified correctly. If the attribution system itself has a gap, MKT's real contribution may be significantly understated — fix tracking first, rather than concluding the campaign didn't work.
  • Launch earlier, with higher visibility: for the next major tournament (Copa América, the Euros), campaigns should go live at least two weeks earlier, with the entry point placed in the primary bet-slip flow or app homepage rather than relying on a promo-page banner alone.
  • Reconcile actual spend against attributed results: given the low share credited to paid teams, compare actual finance spend against Looker's attribution to rule out "spend happened but wasn't tracked."
  • Switch to an incremental metric: next time, use "World Cup window turnover vs. the same period last year outside a major tournament" as the north star, rather than "total platform turnover during the event window" — this better isolates the campaign's real contribution instead of being diluted by baseline volume.

The Target Itself May Never Have Been Aligned

At this period's 35-day rate of $322M (Valid TO), the annualized run-rate comes out to roughly $3.36B / year — meaning the platform's overall scale already sits well above 1B. But 35 days is only 9.6% of the year; compressing a full year's order of magnitude into a single tournament window meant it was arithmetically out of reach from day one. That points to something more important than any single lens's achievement rate: "1B" was likely never clearly defined as either "absolute volume during the event" or "the annual uplift the event drives."

What we'd do next time:

  1. Align on the target first, before any execution — before the next major tournament kicks off, align with whoever set the "1B" goal on what it actually means: absolute volume during the event, the annual uplift the event drives, or simply a rallying cry. Design a genuinely achievable, attributable KPI from there (e.g. "X% growth over the same period last year" or "Y% of turnover from new customers acquired during the event").
  2. Zero-budget, high-leverage product moves — increase the visibility of the World Cup entry point on the app/site homepage during the event (fully within Product's control, no incremental budget required), and A/B test the bet-slip flow to reduce drop-off — this ships faster than waiting on additional MKT budget.
  3. Fix the attribution foundation before judging impact — before the next campaign launches, make sure Event/Source tags fully cover all World Cup–related traffic, including players who arrived via a WC entry point but ultimately bet using a standard bonus. The current tracking gap makes it hard to accurately measure either MKT's real contribution or Product's impact.
  4. Stay the course on localization-first — VND/THB/INR/CNY remain the largest markets (over 90% of Valid TO combined); language and cultural localization around the tournament carries more leverage than incremental ad spend, especially given paid teams' sub-2.1% attributed contribution — organic reach and product experience are the real growth engine.
  5. Break the target into staged sub-goals — if we take on a similar-magnitude target again, split it into "pre-tournament baseline," "tournament window," and "post-tournament carry-over (D7/D30 retention)" phases, each with its own achievable sub-goal — this avoids one ambiguous headline target being interpreted differently by every team.

2026 World Cup Hub Performance Review (FINAL v2)

Updated after re-checking the numbers against the UI/UX team's final deck; every change from the prior version is flagged in the text
UX behavior data: 2026-05-28–07-20 (GA4+Clarity, UI/UX team's final deck) | Hot Games financials: 2026-05-26–07-20 (Prod DB, Admin basis, covers finals weekend)

Bottom line: the real story here isn't how much money World Cup Hub made. It's that this is the first time any of our four brands (OT/12bet, Nova88, Goal123, Bwing) has built an actual dedicated zone for a major tournament like the World Cup or Euro Cup. Every previous tournament, our highest-earning matches never got a dedicated entry point or dedicated content: the traffic and the revenue just sat scattered across whatever pages happened to carry them, and nobody pulled it together into something that could be tracked and improved over time.

Hub drove engagement and deposit intent, and the mobile-first design turned out to be the right call. Hot Games barely moved during finals weekend (Jul 18–20), confirming the tournament's momentum had already faded after the group stage. That, along with the stuck Thailand conversion rate and revenue riding on a handful of players, is what needs fixing first in the next version. Hub isn't making much money yet; its value is still in driving traffic and waking up dormant accounts. The next move is connecting that traffic to Sportsbook, which is where the company actually makes money off it.

Core Events & Conversion Metrics Overview

Everyone across the company uses these numbers. They're pulled straight from the UI/UX team's final "WC26 UX & Behavioral Insights Report" deck (analysis window 2026-05-28–07-20, all counts deduplicated) and unchanged here. Everything in the sections below builds on them.

Hub-visitor figures went through three versions; UXI has since corrected them by phone/email

The final deck extended the window from 05-28–07-16 to 05-28–07-20 to include finals week, and most numbers only moved up slightly. One figure didn't: the deck's own "Hub visitors" row showed OT jumping from 30,477 to 76,872, a 152% spike, while Nova88 barely moved, 21,561 to 24,431, up 13%. Four extra days of data can't explain a 1.5x jump on one brand and almost nothing on the other, so we raised it with UXI directly. In other words, the earlier reading that "Nova88 had actually pulled ahead" was a false signal from a slide error, not a real reversal. The interpretation below uses the corrected figures.

Data Analyst's take: the two brands are running different shapes, volume vs. quality, not better vs. worse

By unique visitors, OT's funnel base (1,193 RegisterClicks) is 2.3x Nova88's (518), yet its completion rate (20.3%) trails Nova88's (24.9%) slightly. In plain terms: OT pulls in more people who click register, but fewer of them actually finish the form and open an account. Out of every 100 who click register, roughly 20 make it through on OT versus roughly 25 on Nova88, about the same gap as the prior version.

The Hub-visitor numbers, recalculated with the corrected figures, tell the same story as before: OT's total site visits (25,606) are 1.3x Nova88's (19,437), and its RegisterClick conversion rate (4.7%) is 1.7x Nova88's (2.7%). So OT isn't just bigger. It's also better at getting visitors to click register in the first place. Where OT actually gets stuck is the form itself, after the click. This direction matches the prior version; the visitor figures behind it have just been checked with UXI and are more trustworthy now.

Nova88 runs the opposite pattern: fewer visitors bother clicking register, but the ones who do finish at a higher rate than OT. Combined with the completion-rate numbers above, the two brands' funnels are breaking down at completely different points. Neither brand is simply doing better or worse. They have different problems. OT needs to figure out why people click register and then abandon partway through; that's a process bottleneck. Nova88 needs more visitors willing to click register in the first place; that's a top-of-funnel intent problem. Holding both to the same bar, say a 25% completion rate, would bury OT's real process issue and misallocate resources. Each brand needs its own fix, not a shared target.

The ratio of deposit clicks to completed registrations is 5.7x for OT and 10.3x for Nova88, almost unchanged from before. This number matters: on both brands, the bulk of Hub traffic is returning members, not net-new users, and Nova88 skews even harder that way. Right now, Nova88's Hub functions more as a reactivation tool for existing players than an acquisition channel. That shapes how the UX, PO, and Marketing sections below should be read.

08UI/UX

Reflections: What to Fix Going Forward

What follows is my own reading and set of ideas as the UX role, working from the data in Sections 01–07, not a restatement of the deck's own narrative. The numbers come from the deck and our own data; the judgment calls and recommendations are mine.

1 · Mobile-first was the right call; the next question is finer-grained

Reading

The mobile-specific header CTA design got mobile to a 20.1%/25.4% completion rate. Mobile-first was the right strategic direction. The final deck adds a number that backs this up further: mobile visitors make up 88% of total Hub traffic, and desktop, which never got this button, sees clicks near zero as expected. Resources went almost entirely to the right device.

But being right on strategy doesn't mean every detail is dialed in: 75 to 80% of people who click register still never finish the form, and there's real room left there. The question now isn't "should we build for mobile," it's "which field on the mobile form is losing the most people."

IdeaNext step is a field-level abandonment analysis: find exactly which field in the form is where people bail, and put the effort there.

2 · Thailand's "high clicks, low completion" problem looks like the same issue on both brands; it isn't

Reading

Breaking down OT's Thailand traffic shows that 59% of its 696 clicks came from elitegame12.com, and that channel converts at only 8.8%, versus 27%+ for every other source (direct/tiktok/google). It's not the form, and it's not localization. It's that this particular channel brings in people who were never that likely to finish the form. A Thai-form health check can wait.

The final deck adds a detail that changes the picture: Nova88's Thailand traffic has no such channel concentration, 72% of it is direct, yet its completion rate is just as bad (21.1%). So OT's Thailand problem has an identifiable culprit; Nova88's low Thailand conversion comes from organic traffic that simply doesn't convert well on its own. Same symptom, two different diagnoses.

Idea

For OT, figure out what's actually driving elitegame12.com's traffic. Is the audience simply a poor fit for the target customer, or is the creative promising something the landing page doesn't deliver (say, a headline about a limited-time offer with nothing to back it up)? The fix is completely different depending on which one it is: cut spend in the first case, tighten up creative-to-landing-page consistency in the second.

For Nova88, since there's no channel to blame, go check the Thai-language landing page and form itself for gaps OT doesn't have. Don't let OT's lower priority on a Thai-form health check carry over to Nova88; the two brands need separate treatment here.

3 · Deposit and register CTAs may be competing for the same visual weight, and the two user types are getting in each other's way

ReadingDeposit clicks vastly outnumber completed registrations. One likely reason: the deposit CTA is already more prominent and easier to hit than the register CTA, so existing members click it without a second thought, while genuine new users can't find a clear entry point buried among content built for returning members.
Idea

Show a different above-the-fold layout depending on login state: logged-out visitors should see "register" as the first CTA, logged-in members should see "deposit."

Split by login state instead of putting two very different intents in front of the same visual elements.

4 · Static page content may explain why match days don't drive noticeably more traffic, and why finals week's small bounce didn't go anywhere

Reading

Looking at group-stage vs. semifinal-stage averages, OT's daily RegisterClick dropped from 43.9 to 13.5, down 69%; Nova88's dropped from 17.8 to 13.5, down 24%. Match days showed no meaningfully higher traffic than off days, tournament excitement isn't converting into repeat visits.

The final deck adds finals week (07/14–07/20): OT climbed back to 23.0/day, Nova88 to 14.9/day, both a brief uptick, both still well below the group-stage peak. That bounce never showed up in Section 05's Hot Games betting numbers. If the page really has stayed static content-wise, that would explain why even a bigger topic like the final itself couldn't turn visitor interest into actual betting, when a final should, in theory, have more conversion potential than any single group-stage match day. Whether page content updates alongside the schedule, hero images, countdowns, featured-match cards, may be part of why. That's my own read, not something the deck tested.

IdeaFirst confirm whether the homepage has a "today's matches" module at all. If not, add one: today's fixtures, live scores, a countdown, something concrete for match-day interest to land on, instead of relying entirely on outside buzz to drive traffic in.

5 · We still can't tell how much channel quality vs. page experience is driving conversion

ReadingCompletion rates vary by more than 3x across channels (elitegame12.com at 8.8%, other sources above 27%), which means blending everything into one all-channel completion rate hides that gap entirely. We can't see how much page experience and traffic quality are each contributing.
Idea

Future analysis and A/B tests should be broken out by channel, not averaged across all of them.

A given page redesign might show no visible lift on a high-completion channel, since those visitors were converting anyway. The real payoff might show up on a low-completion channel instead.

09Product Owner

Overview: Making World Cup Hub Work Better Next Time

Focused on where this feature itself should go next, pulling together the findings from the sections above into a prioritized list.

Positioning

Hub is a traffic/reactivation tool, and its value sits at the top of the funnel and in reactivating the existing base

Hot Games revenue ($11K on 12bet, $4K on Nova88, $15K combined) is still small next to the company's overall platform, and finals weekend barely moved it.

Hub's real value is how much engagement and deposit intent it drives. That's the right way to judge it, not the Hot Games revenue figure on its own.

Next-round resource priority

Fix Thailand's conversion before pouring in more acquisition traffic

Thailand already has the biggest, strongest traffic and intent: it's missing only the last mile, the form experience. Fixing that will beat pouring more traffic into a funnel with a known-low conversion rate.

Product to-do list for the next tournament (Asian Cup, Euro, etc.)
1

Tracking hygiene: every funnel step needs to tie back to an account

KnockoutSwipe, FloatingSoccer, and Promotion clicks are all anonymous right now, so there's no way to prove any lift for existing members. Next time, attach a user_id or UserName parameter across the board so logged-in state is trackable end to end.

2

Tie Hub content more tightly to Sportsbook

Hub content (Hot Games, Promotions) is basically its own little playground right now, loosely connected to the company's real business, sportsbook. The next version needs a clear, trackable path from Hub into Sportsbook, which is the key to connecting Hub's traffic-driving power to the company's actual revenue.

3

Launch earlier, extend the warm-up window, and let content shift with the tournament stage

This time, launch and peak traffic both landed during the group stage, with not enough warm-up beforehand and no content shift once interest dropped afterward. Next time, launch two to three weeks earlier, with content that automatically shifts by tournament stage.

4

Fully separate the new-user funnel from existing-member reactivation

The gap between deposit clicks and completed registrations proves these are two entirely different user intents. Page design, dashboards, and marketing campaigns next time should treat them as two separate tracks instead of blending them together.

5

Re-evaluate Hot Games selection and build a live dashboard

Avoid over-selecting high-RTP, low-hold titles that make revenue dependent on a single player. Worth noting: this round's country, device, and channel analysis all happened after the tournament ended. A live dashboard would let a stuck conversion rate in Thailand, or any similar issue, get fixed while the tournament is still running instead of surfacing only after the fact.

6

Add visual weight to the register CTA, paired with tournament-countdown copy

Increase the visual prominence of the homepage's floating button and add limited-time countdown copy ("World Cup — last chance for this bonus"). Same direction as the fix in item 2 about the deposit CTA burying the new-user entry point.

7

Daily missions with freebets, plus a Hub-to-Sportsbook betting incentive: turn a one-off spike into repeat visits and cross-category spend

Tournament momentum fades fast after the group stage (OT daily RegisterClick down 69% from group stage to semifinal week, Nova88 down 24%). Finals week saw a small bounce (OT back to 23.0/day, Nova88 to 14.9/day), but none of it converted into Hot Games betting, so topic buzz alone clearly isn't enough to move people anymore, and Hub still isn't connected to Sportsbook (see item 2). Two layers of incentive could address both problems at once.

The first is a daily mission: log into Hub, check today's match cards, place one bet on any Hot Games title, and the completed mission pays out a small freebet, turning a one-off tournament moment into a daily habit instead of relying purely on schedule-driven excitement.

The second is a Hub-to-Sportsbook incentive: players who enter Sportsbook through Hub and bet on a World Cup match get boosted odds or bet insurance (a partial refund on a loss), which directly converts Hub's traffic into the company's actual high-value betting category.

Both mechanics depend on the tracking hygiene from item 1 to attribute entry_source; freebets and bet insurance need hard caps and wagering requirements to prevent abuse. Once live, measure the net lift against existing events like DepositClick and CompleteRegistration as a baseline, so results are visible while the tournament is still running instead of only after it ends.

10Marketing

Assessing the Shortfall: Scenarios, Assumptions, and Next Steps

World Cup Hub's current promotion is mostly short-form video for general awareness, with no targeted paid advertising for individual features. What follows is my own read as the Marketing role, working from that reality. The focus is on which promotional judgment calls the numbers might be exposing as wrong, not restating the numbers themselves or the deck's own conclusions.

1

Short-video reach might not be converting into actual new users

If short-video performance is judged by views and likes/shares, the vanity numbers can look great without telling us how many people actually completed registration.

Deposit clicks run 5.7 to 10.1x completed registrations, which shows Hub itself isn't bringing in that many new users. If short-video content is also mostly reaching the existing audience, the real net-new number is smaller than the reach figures suggest.

Assumption: if short-video reporting only tracks views and engagement rate without tying back to downstream actions like RegisterClick or CompleteRegistration, it's probably overstating this push's actual contribution to acquisition.

Next time: short-video performance needs to be attributed against completed registration or further downstream, not just reach metrics. Even if short-video itself isn't a precision-targeting tool, campaign codes should still track how much of that traffic eventually converts to new users.

2

Thailand's short-video reach looks great; nobody's checking the completion rate

Thailand has the highest CTR in the whole campaign (2.68%). If short-video performance there is judged by reach, views, and CTR, the market looks like a clear win on paper.

Assumption: looking only at reach and CTR while ignoring completion rate rewards a market with the campaign's worst completion rate (16.0%) with even more short-video budget. Thailand keeps getting treated as a priority market, and results still aren't scaling with it. That's probably part of why.

Next time: Thailand's short-video performance evaluation needs to pair reach and clicks with completion rate, checked regularly while the campaign is running, not discovered as a gap only after the tournament ends.

3

elitegame12.com drove 65% of Thailand's clicks, and converts worse than any other channel

Breaking down Thailand's traffic sources shows elitegame12.com contributing 65% of clicks at just an 8.8% completion rate, the worst of any channel; every other source (direct/tiktok/google) converts above 27%.

Assumption: judging this channel only on clicks or CTR (5.02%) makes it look solid; completion rate tells the opposite story. If this is paid traffic, budget may have been going into a channel with genuinely poor conversion quality the whole time. If it's an organic referral partnership, it suggests the audience just isn't a good fit for the target customer.

Next time: whether it's paid or an organic partnership, any renewal or continued relationship needs to be judged on completion rate, not clicks. For a known low-converter like elitegame12.com, figure out first whether it's a traffic-quality issue or a promise-vs-landing-page gap before deciding what to do about it.

4

Short-video pacing looks reactive to the schedule rather than planned ahead of it

Quantified against group-stage vs. semifinal-stage averages: OT's daily RegisterClick down 69%, Nova88 down 24%. W25, peak group-stage intensity, was the high point for both metrics, followed by a steady decline. If short-video publishing and paid boosting followed a flat schedule across the whole tournament, effort stayed level even as traffic was already dropping off, and that inefficiency was baked in.

Assumption: if short-video publishing or boosting decisions were made reactively, week to week, rather than planned against the tournament calendar ahead of time, that means underinvesting during the traffic peak (missing the best conversion window) and overinvesting during the lulls (spending on low-efficiency stretches). Hot Games betting barely moved during finals weekend, just $298 combined GGR over three days, which confirms the acquisition window really had already closed.

Next time: plan short-video release and boosting against the historical heat curve for each tournament stage (group stage, knockouts, finals) ahead of time, and only fine-tune once the event starts. Deciding next week's push based on this week's numbers is already a step behind.

5

If any short-video content featured a big Hot Games win, that may be a one-off that won't repeat

Nearly all of 12bet Hot Games' margin came from one player's single bet on 6/7 (GGR $11,544); take it out and 12bet actually runs a loss of $499. No comparable big bettor showed up again during the finals.

Big-win footage is a common short-video format. If any content featured or referenced a large payout from these titles, it's probably using exactly this kind of number, which isn't likely to happen again.

Assumption: packaging a single outlier as the pitch might grab clicks short-term, but the gap between that footage and what most players actually experience can damage trust in these games, and in the Hub campaign as a whole, hurting long-term retention.

Next time: any short-video content about Hot Games payouts should represent the real, broad distribution of outcomes (like Nova88's genuinely small, spread-out wins) instead of any single large case, so the impression matches what most players actually experience.
